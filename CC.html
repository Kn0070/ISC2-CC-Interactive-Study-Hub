1. Incident Response (IR) Lifecycle

The IR process is a six-phase cycle that begins long before an attack. Click each phase below to see its description.

1. Preparation

This is the establishment of policies, procedures, tools, and training needed to handle an incident. Without this stage, all subsequent steps will fail.

2. Detection and Analysis

Determining if an event is a security incident and gathering data about it.

3. Containment

Limiting the scope and impact of the incident (e.g., isolating a compromised system).

4. Eradication

Eliminating the root cause of the incident (e.g., patching the exploited vulnerability).

5. Recovery

Restoring the affected systems and services to normal operation.

6. Post-Incident Activity (Lessons Learned)

Documenting what happened and improving procedures to prevent recurrence.

2. Business Continuity vs. Disaster Recovery

Business Continuity (BC)

Focuses on the business side. Its primary goal is to maintain the critical functions of the organization during and immediately after a disruption. It ensures the business keeps operating.

Disaster Recovery (DR)

Focuses on the technology side. Its goal is the technical restoration of IT infrastructure and systems that support the business. DR is a component of the overall BC plan.

3. Access Control and Least Privilege

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)

The most common enterprise model. Access permissions are grouped into Roles (e.g., 'HR Manager', 'IT Technician'), and users are assigned these roles. This simplifies management and ensures consistency.

Principle of Least Privilege

A fundamental security concept. Users and processes should only be granted the minimum level of access (rights, permissions, privileges) required to perform their authorized tasks, and nothing more.
